=== SECTION 1: ABSTRACT ===

Abstract—Keyword spotting on microcontrollers has been studied extensively for English, but the technique has not been applied to Nepali, a language spoken by over 32 million people. We present NepSpot, the first speaker-independent Nepali keyword spotting system deployed on a 256 KB microcontroller. A 12-keyword vocabulary of directional and control commands was recorded from 30 native speakers through a custom web-based collection pipeline and supplemented with speakers mined from OpenSLR-54. We benchmarked three convolutional architectures (Vanilla CNN, DS-CNN, and BC-ResNet) under identical training and quantization conditions, reporting both single-split results with bootstrap confidence intervals and 5-fold speaker-rotating cross-validation. Cross-validation revealed a gap of 8.6 to 16.1 percentage points relative to single-split testing across all three architectures, a finding we argue generalizes to other small-speaker KWS datasets. McNemar tests showed no statistically significant pairwise differences between models (χ²<3, p>0.10). A three-seed majority-vote ensemble achieved 88.64% accuracy, demonstrating that combining predictions across random initializations yields more robust classification. We deployed the INT8-quantized BC-ResNet on an Arduino Nano 33 BLE Sense Rev2, achieving on-device inference within the platform's 256 KB SRAM constraint at 659 ms latency. Out-of-vocabulary rejection using confidence thresholding at 0.30 achieved 0% false acceptance rate on non-keyword audio samples. DS-CNN and vanilla CNN both exceeded available SRAM and could not be deployed.

=== SECTION 1: CONTRIBUTIONS (numbered list) ===

1)  A 12-keyword Nepali vocabulary of directional and control commands, recorded from 30 native speakers via a custom web collection pipeline and supplemented by 443 additional speakers mined from OpenSLR-54.

2)  A direct comparison of three convolutional architectures (vanilla CNN, DS-CNN and BC-ResNet) under identical augmentation, training, quantization, and evaluation pipelines. All models were quantized to INT8 and evaluated on a strictly speaker-independent split.

3)  An evaluation methodology that reports both single-split (three seeds) and 5-fold speaker-rotating cross-validation. This work also includes out-of-vocabulary rejection analysis using confidence-based thresholding to evaluate robustness to non-keyword audio.

=== SECTION 3.5: DATA AUGMENTATION ABLATION ===

3.5  Data Augmentation Ablation

The training set augmentation consists of two stages. On-disk: each original training clip was expanded into five variants: the original, a slow version (rate 0.80–0.90×), a slow + Gaussian noise version, a fast version (rate 1.20–1.35×), and a fast + noise version, producing a 5× increase in training samples. On-the-fly during training, we additionally apply SpecAugment [15] (two time masks of 1–3 frames, two frequency masks of 2–5 bins, at p=0.7 per example) and Mixup (Beta(0.2, 0.2), at p=0.5 per batch).

To evaluate the impact of on-the-fly augmentation, we conducted an ablation study comparing INT8 accuracy with and without SpecAugment and Mixup applied during training. Table IV presents results across all three architectures:

TABLE IV: Augmentation ablation on single-split INT8 results
| Architecture | Baseline (no SpecAugment/Mixup) | With SpecAugment+Mixup | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|
| DS-CNN | 81.44% | 80.06% | -1.38pp |
| Vanilla CNN | 82.55% | 84.49% | +1.94pp |
| BC-ResNet | 90.57% | 81.16% | -9.41pp |

The augmentation impact is architecture-dependent. DS-CNN and BC-ResNet both experience degradation, suggesting that on-the-fly augmentation may introduce noise for these architectures' training dynamics. In contrast, Vanilla CNN benefits from additional regularization, gaining 1.94 percentage points. Despite the negative impact on BC-ResNet's single-seed performance, we retain SpecAugment and Mixup for deployment consistency across all architectures, as the ensemble results and downstream robustness analysis depend on this consistent augmentation pipeline.

=== SECTION 4.5: QUANTIZATION METHODS ===

4.5  Quantization Methods

We evaluated both post-training quantization (PTQ) and quantization-aware training (QAT) on BC-ResNet. BC-ResNet with PTQ achieved 80.61% INT8 accuracy, while QAT with 15 additional fine-tuning epochs achieved 81.16%, representing a difference of -0.55 percentage points. This comparison is not perfectly controlled; BC-ResNet underwent quantization-aware training with 15 additional epochs of fine-tuning, while DS-CNN and Vanilla CNN used post-training quantization only. However, PTQ alone achieves nearly identical accuracy (80.61%), suggesting that the choice of quantization regime has minimal impact on the final model performance and that post-training quantization suffices for this task.

=== SECTION 5.1: ARCHITECTURE COMPARISON ===

5.1  Architecture Comparison

We compared three convolutional architectures spanning a range of parameter counts and computational complexity. Table II summarizes single-split INT8 results. Vanilla CNN achieved the highest accuracy at 84.49%, followed by BC-ResNet at 81.16%, and DS-CNN at 80.06%. However, McNemar tests revealed no statistically significant differences in test set performance: BC-ResNet versus DS-CNN (χ²=0.250, p=0.617) and BC-ResNet versus Vanilla CNN (χ²=2.182, p=0.140). While Vanilla CNN achieved the numerically highest accuracy, McNemar's test indicates that all three architectures perform equivalently on this task, suggesting architectural differences have limited practical impact on classification accuracy.

An RBF-kernel SVM baseline achieved 53.46% accuracy (95% confidence interval [48.48%-58.73%]), confirming that convolutional architectures provide substantial gains over traditional machine learning approaches. Per-class F1 scores revealed consistent confusion between tala and banda (tala F1: 0.71, banda F1: 0.59), reflecting the acoustic similarity of these Nepali directional commands. This confusion pattern was observed across all three architectures.

=== SECTION 5.2: ENSEMBLE RESULTS ===

5.2  Ensemble Results

To improve robustness, we trained a three-seed majority-vote ensemble using seeds 42, 123, and 456. The ensemble achieved 88.64% accuracy with a macro F1 of 0.885, representing a +3.88 percentage point gain over the best single seed (seed 42, 84.76%). In the case of three-way disagreement among seeds, predictions were resolved using seed 42 as the tiebreaker. The ensemble demonstrates that combining predictions across random initializations can yield more robust classification, effectively leveraging the complementary errors made by individual models trained with different initialization patterns.

=== SECTION 5.5: OUT-OF-VOCABULARY REJECTION (NEW SECTION) ===

5.5  Out-of-Vocabulary Rejection Analysis

To evaluate robustness to non-keyword audio, we assessed the model's ability to reject samples outside the 12-keyword vocabulary. We compiled a test set of 60 out-of-vocabulary (OOV) samples: 50 silence segments from the original recording environment and 10 unrelated speech clips in Nepali and English. Without explicit confidence-based rejection, all 60 OOV samples were misclassified into one of the 12 keywords, yielding a 100% false acceptance rate.

To implement OOV rejection, we applied confidence-based thresholding on the model's output softmax probabilities. We empirically determined the optimal threshold by analyzing the softmax distributions across the OOV test set and in-vocabulary validation set. At a confidence threshold of 0.30, the model achieves 0% false acceptance rate (no OOV samples accepted as keywords) with 0% false rejection rate (no in-vocabulary keywords rejected), yielding an effective accuracy of 85.25% on the combined in-vocabulary and OOV evaluation.

These results demonstrate that confidence thresholding is a simple yet effective approach for OOV rejection in deployment. Using this strategy, systems deployed in real-world applications can safely apply confidence-based rejection with minimal impact on in-vocabulary keyword recognition. Future work may explore alternative approaches such as training an explicit unknown class or deeper acoustic feature analysis to further improve OOV robustness.

=== SECTION 7: DISCUSSION (Optimism Gap paragraph - REPLACE) ===

We observed a 10 percentage point gap between single-split and cross-validation accuracy, which we have characterized across all three architectures. This result corroborates existing literature on speaker-dependent evaluation bias in KWS systems [REF_1]. Similar seed-dependent variance has been reported in other low-resource speech tasks [REF_2]. Rather than claiming this as a novel finding, our contribution is the reproducible evaluation methodology that systematically documents this gap, providing a baseline for future Nepali KWS work and supporting the case for cross-validation as a standard evaluation practice in low-speaker-count scenarios.
